Public Intervention: Stabilization Policies

From Bénassy-Quéré (2019, pg. 25)

Stabilisation policies are aimed at bringing the economy closer to balance (full employment together with price stability) vis-à-vis macroeconomic shocks.

Keynes gave two reasons for such intervention: - “animal spirits”: instability of private behavior under the influence of spontaneous expectations - nominal rigidities of wages and prices prevent the self-correcting market mechanisms from operating and moving the economy back to equilibrium

In the eyes of Keynes, the combination of private instability and ineffective self-correcting mechanisms provided a justification for relying on countercyclical monetary and fiscal policies to smooth out economic fluctuations and prevent economic depressions.

The theory of real business cycles developed in the 1980s was a conceptually coherent attempt at explaining fluctuations by shocks to the production technology (or productivity shocks) and rational responses to them by maximizing agents thus without relying in any significant way on irrational behavior or nominal rigidities. However, in spite of the considerable literature devoted to this approach, its empirical relevance for the explanation of short-term fluctuations remains disputed.

Of the two explanations offered by Keynes, the first—the notion that economic agents are driven by “animal spirits” rather than by cool-headed rational calculation—was and remains in contradiction with the basic assumptions of economics. The argument based on nominal rigidities is theoretically closer to mainstream economics, provided an explanation is given for why and how such rigidities exist and affect economic behavior. Before, only ad-hoc explanation existed for the rigidity (irrational agents enter into nominally rigid contracts), in the 1980s Keynesian economists provided micro-founded explanation: microeconomic agents act rationally because the benefit of changing prices is smaller compared to macroeconomic benefit.

The standard, contemporary approach is based on a simple aggregate supply and demand framework that depicts the relationship between potential output and the product price, on the one hand, and between aggregate product demand and the product price, on the other. In the short run (in the presence of nominal rigidities) aggregate supply depends positively on the product price because a rise in the price level reduces the real wage and makes the production more profitable. In the long run aggregate supply is fixed as unemployment is at its equilibrium, so the curve is vertical. Aggregate demand depends negatively on the price, as a rise in the price reduces the real value of nominal assets and thereby reduces the consumption (see the book for formal derivation).

Supply shock: exogenous modification in the relation between potential output and the product price. An oil shock (a rise in the price of oil) reduces the level of potential output because it increases prices and reduces the profitability of production, supply curve shifts to the left.

Demand shock: exogenous modification in the relationship between product demand and the product price. For example, a drop in the level of household consumption resulting from a reduction of household wealth, demand curve shifts to the left.

They command different policy responses. A simple criterion for distinguishing demand from supply shocks therefore is that they result in opposite changes in the price for a similar effect on output (see plots in the book for short-run and long-run effects). The upshot is that a demand shock either does not affect output or moves it in the same direction as price, while a supply shock moves price in the opposite direction to that of output.

This framework helps to explain the role and the limits of stabilization. A fiscal or monetary impulse affects the demand curve and can therefore offset a demand shock. This is, for example, the elementary reasoning behind the response of governments of advanced economies to the depression of 2009: as confidence dropped and household wealth declined, macroeconomic policy aimed to stimulate private demand through lower interest rates and direct transfers to households. However, a fiscal or monetary impulse does not affect the aggregate supply curve so neither is effective in response to a supply shock. f the supply curve shifts to the left in response to a rise in the relative price of oil (which makes other products less profitable and therefore reduces supply), pushing aggregate demand to the right necessarily results in a further increase in the price level and is fully ineffective in the long run. Therefore, demand policies are not effective in response to supply shocks. The latter call for supply policies.

The effectiveness of demand policies depends on the slope of the short-term supply curve. In an economy with widespread nominal rigidities and a low responsiveness of wages to labor market conditions, the short-run supply curve can be almost flat, which makes demand policies very effective. However, when indexation is fast and wages are responsive to unemployment, the slope of the short-run supply curve can be almost vertical, rendering demand policies close to ineffective. The choice of a proper policy response thus depends both on the identification of shocks and on the underlying properties of the economy.

In real time, however, policymakers are generally unable to identify the nature of the shocks and to evaluate the slopes of the supply and demand curves. For example, they observe a rise in the price level but do not know whether it represents a normal response to shocks to input prices (e.g., oil) or results from a change in agents’ behaviors (such as a change in price expectations or indexation mechanisms).
